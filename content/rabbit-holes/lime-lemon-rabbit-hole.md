### Classification
Classification is the human practice of sorting and naming things according to shared traits, visible patterns, observed behaviors, and recurring forms. It helps people make sense of the world by grouping what appears related, yet it can also create confusion when surface features are mistaken for deeper identity. In child-led discovery, classification becomes more than memorizing categories; it becomes a way of testing whether what we assume actually holds up under closer observation.

{% anchor "Child-Led-Inquiry" %}
### Child-Led Inquiry
Child-led inquiry is the process by which a child’s own question, interest, confusion, or fascination becomes the engine of learning. Rather than moving through information because it was assigned, the child follows a living trail of meaning, deciding what to test, compare, notice, and ask next. This form of inquiry often produces stronger engagement, deeper memory, and more integrated understanding because the learning grows from genuine internal motivation rather than external demand.

{% anchor "Curiosity" %}
### Curiosity
Curiosity is the force that turns an ordinary moment into an opening. It begins when something does not quite fit — a yellow lime in a bowl, a strange smell, a mismatch between expectation and reality — and instead of being dismissed, it is followed. Curiosity is often the true beginning of unschooling, because it transforms confusion into motion and invites the learner into an active relationship with the world.

{% anchor "Evidence" %}
### Evidence
Evidence consists of the clues gathered through direct experience, comparison, testing, observation, and research before deciding what is true. It may come through smell, taste, shape, texture, source, pattern, or repeated confirmation rather than assumption alone. In natural learning, evidence teaches children that certainty becomes stronger when it is built from what can be noticed, checked, and questioned rather than what is simply declared.

{% anchor "Identity-and-Change" %}
### Identity and Change
Identity and change hold the tension between what shifts on the surface and what remains fundamentally the same. A thing may alter in color, size, texture, age, or stage without becoming something entirely different, and learning to notice that distinction reveals a deeper understanding of development. This concept reaches far beyond fruit, opening questions about biology, growth, maturity, personhood, and the way people often confuse transformation in appearance with transformation in essence.

{% anchor "Kitchen-Lab" %}
### Kitchen Lab
A kitchen lab is an everyday household space transformed into a site of experimentation, comparison, and discovery. It is where the ordinary materials of life — fruit, cups, spoons, smells, textures, seeds, scraps, heat, and questions — become enough to begin real investigation. In unschool life, the kitchen lab reminds us that learning does not require a formal classroom in order to be rigorous, meaningful, or memorable.

{% anchor "Living-Record" %}
### Living Record
A living record is the captured trail of thought, observation, questions, notes, mistakes, and discoveries that shows learning as it actually unfolds. It preserves process rather than only polished conclusion, making visible the path from assumption to revision to deeper understanding. In The Unschool Pages, a living record matters because it honors learning as something lived, witnessed, and evolving rather than something flattened into final answers alone.

{% anchor "Natural-Learning" %}
### Natural Learning
Natural learning is the process through which understanding grows from life itself — from play, work, wonder, conversation, experimentation, storytelling, and the practical conditions of being alive in the world. It is not separated from ordinary experience but embedded inside it, often appearing wherever attention meets relevance. This kind of learning tends to be durable because it is connected to real context, real questions, and the learner’s own felt need to know.

{% anchor "Observation" %}
### Observation
Observation is the discipline of slowing down enough to notice what is actually present before rushing to explanation. It involves attending to shape, smell, color, size, pattern, behavior, contrast, and change over time, often revealing distinctions that quick labeling misses. Observation is one of the foundational acts of discovery because it trains the learner to see more clearly before deciding what something means.

{% anchor "Rabbit-Hole" %}
### Rabbit Hole
A rabbit hole is a question trail that begins with one small curiosity and unfolds into many connected layers of discovery. A single puzzle may lead into science, language, systems, history, philosophy, daily life, and personal meaning without needing to stay inside one subject boundary. Rabbit holes matter because they reflect how real learning often works: not as neat sequencing, but as living webs of connection pulled open by attention.

{% anchor "Ripening" %}
### Ripening
Ripening is the process by which fruit changes over time in color, texture, smell, flavor, and softness as it matures. These changes can be dramatic enough to alter how the fruit looks to the casual eye, yet the fruit remains what it is even while moving through a new stage of development. Ripening offers a powerful way to study change, because it shows how appearance can shift without identity being replaced.

{% anchor "Taxonomy" %}
### Taxonomy
Taxonomy is a more formal system of classification used in science to organize living things according to relationships, traits, lineage, and structure. It helps distinguish between common names and deeper biological identity, especially in cases where everyday language creates confusion. In a child’s investigation, taxonomy opens the door from “what does this look like?” to “what is this, really, and how do we know?”

{% anchor "Threshold" %}
### Threshold
A threshold is the moment when an easy assumption breaks and a deeper question appears. It is the crossing point between passive acceptance and active investigation, where certainty gives way to curiosity and a learner steps into discovery. Threshold moments are often small and ordinary from the outside, yet they mark the beginning of real intellectual movement.

{% anchor "Witness" %}
### Witness
Witness is the act of noticing, honoring, and sometimes documenting learning as it naturally emerges. It does not force or direct the discovery so much as remain present enough to recognize its significance and preserve its trace. In The Unschool Pages, witness is part observer, part documentarian, and part keeper of the living evidence that learning was here.
