Prologue

For a long time, I could feel that I had something to contribute to the unschooling community, but I had not yet named the shape of it clearly enough to protect it.

Like many people who live outside the standard script, I have seen how quickly the world tries to assign roles. Teacher. Coach. Expert. Debater. Advocate. Corrective voice. Public explainer. But none of these are the truest center of my work.

I am an entertainer.
I am a documentarian.
I am an author of lived possibility.

I am a cognitive scientist, anthropologist, linguist, software engineer, artist, and unschooler. I move through life noticing patterns, meanings, rituals, language, systems, and stories. And what I have noticed, again and again, is how little room our culture makes for vivid representations of unschoolers and worldschoolers living real, expansive, intelligent, unconventional lives outside the cookie-cutter system.

We are too often reduced to stereotypes, fears, debates, and explanations.

But life is richer than argument.

Life is in the small scenes.
The rabbit trails.
The deschooling.
The humor.
The reinvention of time.
The return of personhood.
The wonder that appears when learning is no longer forced to march in a straight line.

That is the territory I want to write from.

Not from the podium, but from within the story.
Not from prescription, but from witness.
Not from the pressure to convince, but from the responsibility to observe, record, and create.

This work is my offering to a global community that is more diverse than ever and still becoming. It is my way of helping without turning life into doctrine. It is my way of contributing without becoming trapped in the narrow roles so often handed to people in alternative education spaces.

I do not want to build a platform around coaching, advising, or debate.

I want to build a body of work.

A body of work that entertains.
A body of work that documents.
A body of work that makes room for unschooling and worldschooling to exist not merely as arguments, but as culture, atmosphere, family life, and unfolding human experience.

Let this stand, then, as a beginning and a boundary.

What you will find here is not a system to obey.
What you will find here is not a perfect model to replicate.
What you will find here is an authored record of discovery, deschooling, lifelong learning, and the many possible textures of a life lived otherwise.

This is the work.
This is the offering.
This is the lane.
