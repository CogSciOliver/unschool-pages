## Women’s Work Is “Technology”

This pattern shows up repeatedly across history. A technical field begins as domestic work, community craft, or clerical labor. Women develop deep expertise and often dominate the workforce. Then the moment the work becomes industrialized, profitable, or strategically powerful, control shifts into institutions owned by wealthy men.

The work itself does not necessarily change. The story about who “owns” the work does.

This isn’t one isolated story. It happens across industries that shape modern life. This pattern that keeps popping up in history is this strange flip: when a field is new, tedious, or underpaid, women dominate it. When it becomes profitable, powerful, or prestigious, institutions reorganize it, **credential it**, and men suddenly dominate it.

This is a useful lens for teaching kids about both technology and history.

## Early Computing: When Women Were the Programmers

Before programming became a high-status career, it was considered routine clerical work.

The earliest programmers were often women.

Earlier still, the word **computer** referred to a human being who performed mathematical calculations. Many of these human computers were women working in observatories, universities, and government labs.

Their work included:

*   Calculating astronomical observations
    
*   Producing ballistics tables for military research
    
*   Computing flight trajectories and engineering data
    
*   Performing complex mathematical analysis by hand
    

When electronic computers appeared in the 1940s, women were hired to program them because the work was considered meticulous clerical labor.

These early programmers helped build the foundations of software engineering:

*   Writing the first programming instructions for electronic machines
    
*   Developing debugging practices
    
*   Designing early program logic and workflows
    
*   Translating mathematical problems into machine operations
    

Despite the technical depth of the work, many were classified as assistants rather than engineers.

Once computing became lucrative and prestigious in the 1970s and 1980s, the demographics of the field shifted dramatically toward men.

## Telephone Operators: The Original Network Engineers

Early telephone networks depended entirely on human operators.

Every call had to be manually connected through a switchboard.

These operators were overwhelmingly women. Companies believed women were more patient and polite, but the work required far more than courtesy.

Operators effectively ran the communication infrastructure of entire regions.

Their work involved:

*   Routing calls across complex communication networks
    
*   Managing traffic loads and line availability
    
*   Troubleshooting connection problems
    
*   Memorizing large switching systems and regional call routes
    

In many ways, these operators were the first large-scale **human network administrators**.

Yet the job was framed as service work rather than technical expertise.

As automated switching technologies replaced human operators, telecommunications engineering and management became male-dominated fields.

### Seeing the Parallel in Modern Infrastructure Work

My comparison between building APIs and being a telephone operator is actually historically accurate.

Both involve:

*   routing information between endpoints
    
*   managing connection logic
    
*   handling errors and retries
    
*   coordinating distributed systems
    

Early telephone networks were literally **manual information routing infrastructure**. Today, as a Software Engineer I build software layers, APIs, gateways, and webhook that perform the same fundamental role, just at machine speed.

In that sense, modern backend infrastructure engineering is a descendant of several earlier kinds of information work that were once performed by large numbers of women.

Understanding that lineage helps explain how technical labor, and who performs it, has shifted repeatedly as technologies evolve.

### Brewing: From Women’s Craft to Industrial Beer

For centuries, brewing beer was primarily women’s work.

Women known as **alewives** brewed beer at home or in small community operations. Brewing required careful knowledge of fermentation, sanitation, timing, and ingredients.

Women’s brewing expertise included:

*   Managing fermentation cycles
    
*   Maintaining yeast cultures
    
*   Experimenting with herbs and flavoring plants
    
*   Producing consistent batches for local communities
    

As brewing industrialized in Europe, commercial breweries formed guilds and regulatory systems that increasingly excluded women.

Brewing shifted from a household craft to a capital-intensive industry controlled largely by male guild members and commercial brewers.

The fermentation science did not disappear. It simply moved into institutions that excluded the women who had practiced it.

### Textile Technology and Cotton

Textile production was one of the most technologically complex crafts in pre-industrial societies.

For centuries, women were central to spinning, weaving, dyeing, and fiber preparation.

Their technical knowledge included:

*   Preparing fibers through carding and cleaning
    
*   Hand spinning with drop spindles and spinning wheels
    
*   Creating complex woven patterns
    
*   Using plant-based chemistry for dyes and pigments
    

When spinning frames and power looms appeared during the Industrial Revolution, textile production moved into factories owned by industrial capitalists.

Women still worked in mills, often under harsh conditions. But control over machinery, engineering design, and factory ownership moved to male industrialists and investors.

The craft knowledge women had maintained for generations became embedded in machines owned by others.

### Food Fermentation: Beer, Bread, and Kombucha

Across many cultures, fermentation traditions were historically maintained by women.

These practices included:

*   Brewing beer
    
*   Baking sourdough bread
    
*   Culturing yogurt and cheese
    
*   Producing fermented beverages like kombucha
    

All of these required practical microbiology long before scientists formally described yeast and bacterial processes.

Women learned through experimentation, observation, and generational knowledge-sharing.

As commercial food industries developed, fermentation moved into laboratories, factories, and regulated production systems controlled by corporations.

The knowledge did not originate in those institutions. Much of it grew out of domestic experimentation and community practice.

## The Credentialing Shift

Another common mechanism in these transitions is **credentialing**.

When knowledge spreads through apprenticeship, community practice, or household traditions, many people can access it.

When industries formalize knowledge through degrees, licensing systems, professional societies, and corporate hierarchies, access becomes restricted.

Historically, many of these institutions excluded women either explicitly or structurally.

This creates a repeating pattern:

*   Women build practical expertise in emerging fields
    
*   The work is labeled domestic, clerical, or low-status
    
*   Industrialization increases the economic value of the field
    
*   Institutions form around the work — factories, corporations, universities, guilds, licensing systems
    
*   Control shifts toward those who control capital, credentials, and infrastructure
    

The knowledge itself does not disappear.  
What changes is who gets recognized as the expert.

- - -

## The Pattern

Across many industries the transition often follows a recognizable arc:

*   A craft or technical field begins as household labor or community knowledge
    
*   Women dominate early stages of the work
    
*   The work is framed as domestic or clerical
    
*   New machines or industrialization increase its economic value
    
*   Institutions form to control production and training
    
*   Control of the field shifts toward wealthy men with capital and political power
    
*   Historical narratives emphasize male inventors while minimizing the women who built the foundations, authored the papers, drew the designs but could not own patents or secure the funding to bring to market.
    

This pattern does not erase women’s contributions.

It simply hides them behind the structures that later claim ownership of the field.

And every once in a while, while wiring together APIs, tunnels, and webhooks, it becomes hard not to notice the echoes.

# Additional Technical Fields Historically Dominated by Women

## Medicine & Healing

Before medicine became institutionalized through universities and licensing systems, healing knowledge was largely maintained by women.

Women served as:
- Midwives
- Herbalists
- Birth attendants
- Community healers
- Pharmacological experimenters using plants and fermentation

They possessed extensive knowledge of anatomy, childbirth, and medicinal plants. When universities began formalizing medical education in Europe between the 1200s–1700s, women were largely excluded from training and licensing.

Male physicians increasingly claimed authority over childbirth and reproductive medicine, fields that had previously been dominated by women.

The professionalization of medicine coincided with the suppression of traditional healers, including during periods of witch persecutions in Europe.

## Pharmaceuticals and Apothecaries

Closely tied to healing work was early pharmacology.
Women historically prepared:
- Tinctures
- Herbal medicines
- Salves
- Fermented remedies
- Distilled plant extracts

Much of this knowledge was household chemistry and botanical science developed through generations of practice.
As chemistry became an academic discipline and pharmaceutical companies emerged in the 18th–20th centuries, medicinal production shifted from domestic laboratories to industrial ones.
The knowledge base often originated in domestic traditions but was later formalized into scientific institutions that largely excluded women.

## Astronomy and Data Analysis

astronomy relied heavily on calculation long before digital computers existed.
Women worked as:
- Mathematical computers
- Observatory assistants
- Astronomical data analysts,
- Catalogers of stars and spectra.
At institutions like the Harvard Observatory in the late 1800s, teams of women analyzed photographic plates of the night sky and created foundational star classification systems.
Despite performing highly technical scientific work, they were typically labeled assistants and paid significantly less than male astronomers.

## Cartography and Mapmaking
Mapmaking historically required extensive data compilation, drawing, and geographic analysis.
Women contributed heavily to:
- Drafting maps,
- Calculating survey measurements,
- Geographic data transcription,
- Map engraving and illustration.
Much of this work was categorized as clerical or artistic rather than technical, even though it required mathematical and geographic expertise.
As modern geospatial science and surveying became professionalized fields tied to engineering and military institutions, male professionals came to dominate the discipline.
